### I am going to attempt the [#30DayMapChallenge](https://twitter.com/hashtag/30DayMapChallenge).

Following Topi Tjukanov's [daily themes](https://github.com/tjukanovt/30DayMapChallenge), every day[^1] throughout the month of November I shall create and publish a different map. 

I'm attempting this potentially disastrous endeavour for two reasons:
# Learning is fun
I recently started a MSc in Geographic Data Science, and at the start of it got to select which modules I would take. One of the "compulsory" modules was a GIS one, but this could be swapped for another module if we felt that we already had sufficient GIS experience (hence the  "compulsory" scare quotes). I opted to do so: the upside being that I now get to take an exciting new module[^2], the downside being that I now find myself bereft of formal GIS instruction, and thus chances to practise, develop, and hopefully even improve my mapping. My intention in committing myself to this challenge is that it gives me a (semi-contrived) reason to keep my GIS skills in shape, and potentially to explore some new data and try some new tools and methods.

# Procrastination/perfectionism/productivity
If my undergraduate degree has taught me anything (in fact it's taught me many things!), it's that I am capable of periods of productivity when faced with a looming deadline, but that when lacking immediate motivation that level of focus is less readily available, and I often lapse into procrastination. In practice this means that I have got increasingly skilled at expeditiously producing high quality work when necessary, but that very few pursuits which lack a time-sensitive element reach completion. It probably doesn't help that I've always been a bit of a perfectionist, and that if given the opportunity I will rarely stop working on something until either I deem it to be unimprovable (a very infrequent occurrence), or I run out of time to spend on it (the case for almost everything I "finish" working on). In my head I realise that it's better to make thirty imperfect maps than to spend a month trying to perfect one, so hopefully this can be a good means to force the perfectionist in me to (at least somewhat) accede to this logic in practice.

I'll probably post the results of what I manage to create here once the challenge is over, maybe with some more detailed write-ups if I do anything particularly noteworthy. In the meantime, everything will be posted to my Twitter:
